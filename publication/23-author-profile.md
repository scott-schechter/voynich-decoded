# Author Profile

## Summary

The decoded vocabulary, quadrilingual structure, and systematic absences identify the author as a Jewish artisan apothecary working in the Montpellier-Marseille corridor of southern France between approximately 1250 and 1350. The surviving manuscript, on Italian vellum dated 1404-1438, is a later copy produced after the 1306 expulsion of Jews from France.

## Quadrilingual Vocabulary Distribution

The manuscript's decoded text divides into four languages with no overlap between domains. Each language was learned in a different context and governs a specific category of knowledge.

**Latin (82.9%)** covers professional pharmaceutical prose: all verbs of instruction (prescribes, heat, take, make, approve), all plant names, all quality assessments, the prescriptive framework, and abstract concepts. This is the language of Montpellier medical education, learned from texts.

**Occitan (10.2%)** covers local marketplace vocabulary: trade materials (wool, lard, bath, gold, tin, fish sauce), articles and possessives (at the, a/one, their), 3rd-plural verb endings (-AN instead of Latin -UNT), and sensory verbs (to smell, to plant). Ten converging Occitan phonological features date the composition to Languedoc. This is the language of daily life, learned from the street.

**Hebrew (6.9%)** covers divine authority (God 740×, goddess 282×, beware 671×, household god 156×), celestial terminology (zodiac names, Sefer Yetzirah cosmology, Ibn Ezra astronomical terms), ritual purity (pure, impure), and sacred materials (myrrh, balsam/resin). This is the language of the synagogue and family, learned from prayer and the father's teaching.

**Aramaic (~0.3%)** covers medical terminology (ASHTA/fire-fever, SAMMA/drug, TICHLA/remedy, ASYA/physician), body parts (DAMA/blood, GARMA/bone), color vocabulary (UKMA/black), numbers (CHAD/one, TLATA/three), processing verbs (CHATAM/seal, TACHHAN/grind), workshop vessels (KASA/cup, SUDAR/cloth), and materials (CHALA/vinegar). This is the language of the beit midrash (study house), learned from Talmudic medical passages (Gittin 67b-70a, Shabbat 109b-134a) and Zoharic tradition.

The four languages never substitute for each other. Hebrew handles the sacred and cosmological. Aramaic handles the medical and practical. Latin handles the professional. Occitan handles the commercial. The split reflects four contexts of learning: Latin from books, Occitan from the street, Hebrew from the synagogue, Aramaic from the study house. ## Workshop Master, Not Scholar

The text addresses groups throughout. Second-person plural verbs: NOCETIS ("you all harm" 513×), FACITIS ("you all make" 487×), NOCITIS ("you all are harmed" 426×). Three imperative commands totaling 363 direct orders: RECIPE ("take!" 118×), FAC ("make!" 101×), HABE ("have/hold!" 144×).

673 HEUS ("beware!") warnings appear, 59% in the biological section. Warnings are followed by harm verbs 30% of the time and addressed to groups. Repetitive warning chanting fills the final third of biological pages: call-and-response safety training during dangerous heated procedures.

## Agricultural Practice

The herbal section contains: VER (spring, 41×, concentrated in herbal pages), SARE (to plant, 93×), SEMEN (seed, 19×), HORTUS (garden, 62×), GAN (Hebrew: garden, 4×). Agricultural verbs: LEGO (to gather), SERO (to sow), SICCO (to dry). KEREM (Hebrew: vineyard) appears on astronomical pages. The zodiac section functions as a planting and harvesting calendar.

## Astronomical Timing

TAL (Hebrew: dew, 53×) concentrates in the biological section (24×). Dew collected at specific astronomical times was used in medieval Jewish medical bathing preparations.

The time-based dosage system (1,177 tokens) measures preparation by duration, not weight: HORAM ("for an hour" 111×), DIEM ("for a day" 54×), BIS ("twice" 283×), TERTIO ("thirdly" 86×). This matches the Dioscorides oil maceration protocol. The manuscript has no weight-based dosage units (no libra, uncia, drachma) because oil maceration is measured by time.

## Kabbalistic Theology

DEA (goddess, 282×) dominates the herbal section. DEUS/HEUS dominate the biological section. Zero goddess on biological pages. Zero warnings on herbal pages. The distribution maps to sefirotic theology: Shekhinah (feminine divine presence) in the natural world, Gevurah (judgment/severity) in danger. ## Textual Connections

- **Shem Tov ben Isaac** (Marseille, 1254-64): 41.3% Latin and 35.9% Occitan vocabulary overlap across 739 entries from his medical synonym lists.
- **Sefer Yetzirah**: TELI (celestial dragon/axis mundi) on the cosmological diagram pages. A term specific to this text.
- **Abraham ibn Ezra**: IGGUL (circle/orbit), DAKAH (minute of arc) from his astronomical works.

These place the author within documented Provençal Jewish intellectual circles: the Kabbalistic school of Isaac the Blind (Posquières), the astronomical circle of Jacob ben Machir (Montpellier), and the Marseille medical translation tradition.

## The Surviving Copy

The composition dates to 1250-1350 (Occitan dialect evidence). The surviving manuscript was produced on northern Italian vellum (radiocarbon 1404-1438), roughly a century later. The 1306 expulsion of Jews from France displaced Languedoc communities to northern Italy.

The surviving manuscript is a copy, not the original. Evidence for this: the ornate illustrations and careful calligraphy suggest a commissioned preservation artifact rather than a working notebook. The page-level word order (verb-lines and noun-lines separated) may reflect a copyist preserving words faithfully while losing their intended reading sequence. Some undecoded tokens may be scribal errors introduced during copying.

## Identification

No specific individual has been identified. Jewish artisan apothecaries in 13th-century Languedoc were below the documentation threshold of the historical record, which preserves physicians, translators, and scholars. The author worked in the orbit of documented figures (Shem Tov, Jacob ben Machir, the Posquières Kabbalistic school) but left no trace in the textual record beyond this manuscript.

The absence of a name is consistent with the profile: an artisan-level practitioner, family-trained and guild-excluded, who transmitted knowledge within his community rather than through institutional channels.
