# Hebrew Trade and Celestial Vocabulary in the Undecoded Residue

## Summary

Systematic matching of 5,142 undecoded manuscript tokens against 251 Hebrew terms from celestial, pharmaceutical, and trade vocabulary identifies approximately 712 potential Hebrew tokens (13.8% of the undecoded residue). These span two domains:

1. **Celestial vocabulary** (253 tokens in the astronomical section): zodiac names (74 occurrences), Hebrew month names (46), Sefer Yetzirah cosmological terms (28), and Abraham ibn Ezra astronomical terminology (23).

2. **Trade/pharmaceutical vocabulary** (459 tokens across all sections): workshop tools and vessels (227 occurrences), humoral quality terms (67), sacred materials (64), processing verbs (45), medical terms (22), and plant parts (22).

The distribution is section-specific: celestial Hebrew concentrates in the astronomical folios (f67–f73), while trade Hebrew concentrates in the herbal section (59% of trade matches). This pattern is consistent with a bilingual practitioner who used Hebrew for craft terminology, religious concepts, and astronomical knowledge while writing the formal pharmaceutical text in Latin-Occitan.

## Celestial Vocabulary (Astronomical Section)

### Method

747 unique undecoded tokens from folios f67–f73 were converted to phonetic approximations using the EVA glyph-to-sound mapping, then compared against 124 Hebrew celestial terms from Sefer Yetzirah, Abraham ibn Ezra's *Reshit Chokhmah*, Talmudic astronomical discussions (Berakhot 32b, Shabbat 75a), and Maimonides' *Hilkhot Kiddush HaChodesh*. Matching used consonant-skeleton comparison with Levenshtein distance, allowing for common Hebrew-EVA substitution pairs (KH/K, T/D, S/SH, P/B, Q/K, G/K).

### Significance

The TELI finding is particularly significant. TELI (celestial dragon/axis mundi) is a uniquely Sefer Yetzirah term that does not appear in general Hebrew usage. Its appearance on the cosmological diagram pages (f72v) establishes a direct connection to Jewish mystical cosmology. GEDI (Capricorn) appearing on two different zodiac pages provides folio-specific validation — the right zodiac name on the right zodiac page.

IGGUL (circle/orbit) from Ibn Ezra appears on three separate folios, all within the astronomical section. This is technical astronomical vocabulary from the specific texts a 13th-century Jewish astronomer would have studied.

## Trade and Pharmaceutical Vocabulary (All Sections)

### Method

The same phonetic matching was applied to 4,571 unique undecoded tokens from all manuscript sections, tested against 127 Hebrew trade and pharmaceutical terms from the Mishnah (Kilayim, Sheviit), Talmud (Shabbat, Avodah Zarah), Shem Tov ben Isaac's medical vocabulary, and medieval Hebrew pharmaceutical texts.

### Key Matches

**Processing verbs:**
- `tochon` → TOCHEN (to grind to powder) — herbal section
- `tcheain` → TOCHEN (variant) — herbal section

**Workshop tools:**
- `kodo` → KAD (jar/jug) — herbal section
- `cphealy` → ELI (pestle) — herbal section
- `qoctholy` → KELI (vessel/tool) — herbal section

**Materials:**
- `mar` → MOR (myrrh) or MAR (bitter) — herbal section
- `tshey`/`otshey`/`tshy` → TZORI (balsam/resin) — 21× across biological, recipe, and herbal sections
- `soeom` → SAM (drug/poison) — herbal section

**Humoral qualities:**
- `rcho`/`orcho` → RACH (soft) — herbal section
- `q?or` → KAR (cold) — herbal section
- `lkl` → LACH (moist) — 9×, recipe section

## Interpretation

The Hebrew vocabulary falls into three functional layers:

1. **Sacred/cosmological** — zodiac names, Sefer Yetzirah terms, month names. These reflect the author's Jewish astronomical education and appear in the sections dealing with celestial timing.

2. **Craft/workshop** — vessel names, processing verbs, tool terms. These are the Hebrew words a Jewish artisan would use in daily workshop practice, embedded in Latin pharmaceutical prose.

3. **Assessment/humoral** — hot, cold, moist, soft, bitter. The Galenic four-qualities system expressed in Hebrew rather than Latin, suggesting the author learned humoral theory through Hebrew medical texts (consistent with the Shem Tov tradition) rather than Latin university training.

This three-layer Hebrew presence — cosmological in the zodiac pages, workshop-practical in the herbal pages, and assessment-theoretical throughout — is exactly the profile of a family-trained Jewish artisan apothecary: someone who learned his craft in Hebrew from family and community, his formal pharmacology from Latin texts in the Montpellier tradition, and his astronomical timing from Jewish sources.

## Limitations

- Phonetic matching produces many false positives, especially at distance 1.0. The 712-token estimate includes matches that may be coincidental.
- Hebrew consonant skeletons are short (2-4 consonants), increasing the chance of spurious matches with EVA tokens.
- The tool category (227 occurrences) is inflated by KELI (vessel) and ELI (pestle), which match many short EVA tokens.
- Confirmation requires checking whether matched tokens appear in contextually appropriate positions (e.g., KELI near material nouns, TZORI in preparation contexts).
